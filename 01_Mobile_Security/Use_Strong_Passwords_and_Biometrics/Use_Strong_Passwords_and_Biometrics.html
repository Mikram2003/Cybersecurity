Strong Passwords

  1. Length: Use at least 16 characters. Longer passwords are generally more secure.

  2. Complexity: Include a mix of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols.

  3. Uniqueness: Use a different password for each account to prevent a single breach from compromising multiple accounts.

  4. Avoid using easily guessable information like birthdays or common words.

  5. Consider using a memorable phrase made up of unrelated words.

    (e.g. “HorsePurpleHatRunBay”)


  6. Managers: Use a password manager to generate and store complex passwords securely.

  7. Regular Updates: Change your passwords regularly to reduce the risk of long-term exposure.

  8. Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Enable 2FA for an additional layer of security.

  9. Avoid Reuse: Never reuse passwords across different sites or services.

  10. Security Questions: Choose security questions with answers that are not easily guessable or publicly available.

Biometrics

  1. Unique Traits: Biometrics use unique physical or behavioral traits like fingerprints, facial recognition, or iris scans.

  2. Difficult to Replicate: Biometric data is hard to forge or replicate, making it a secure authentication method.

  3. Convenience: Biometrics provide quick and easy access without the need to remember passwords.

  4. Multi-Factor Authentication: Combining biometrics with passwords enhances security.

  5. Device Integration: Many modern devices support biometric authentication, making it widely accessible.

  6. Reduced Risk of Theft: Unlike passwords, biometric data cannot be easily stolen or shared.

  7. Continuous Authentication: Some systems use continuous biometric authentication to ensure ongoing security.

  8. Privacy Concerns: Be aware of privacy implications and ensure biometric data is stored securely.

  9. Fallback Options: Always have a fallback authentication method in case biometric systems fail.

  10. Regular Updates: Keep biometric systems updated to protect against new vulnerabilities.
